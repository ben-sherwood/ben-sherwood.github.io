A Story about HTML and CSS

03/04/2019

Once upon a time there lived a man named Hamish Tyson Martin-Luther – Quite the long name. He hated it. He lived a fairly normal life, a 9-5 construction job with good pay, working with many different people from all works of life with many different skill sets. He enjoyed what he did, but often felt that his job wasn’t that meaningful. All day he saw his constructions transformed by others into beautiful works of art for all to enjoy. One of his closest friends, Claire Shatner-Summers was an interior designer who he had worked with for years. She was incredible at taking a rather bland concrete wall and creating something beautiful. Hamish dreamed that maybe one day that would be him, and he would escape the grayscale of construction.

One evening after a long hard day, Hamish and Claire were having a drink, when Claire remarked “Hamish, you have seemed a bit down recently, is everything ok?”
“Well… now that you ask, I am feeling pretty low. I have spent my whole life following in my fathers’ footsteps, creating office blocks and abodes, factories and farms, yet their beauty lies not in my handwork, but in the transformation they undergo when a designer works their magic. Why can’t I create art like that?” Hamish looked into the bottom of his pint trying desperately to avoid eye contact. Talking about his emotions was not something he was overly fond of doing.
“You know what Hamish? There is beauty in your work. There is beauty in the organisation of the chaos that is brick and mortar. When I come to design the interior of your buildings, I see geometry. I see nature in the shapes, and without that I cannot do what I do. The designs I make complement the buildings you build, and the buildings complement my design. They are one of the same. We create beauty, together".
Hamish smiled, he hadn’t thought about it like that.

As the sun lay down to rest, Hamish closed his eyes, ready for the day tomorrow and the beauty he would create from chaos.

The End

A guide to Margins, Borders, and Padding

03/04/2019

To understand Margins, borders, and padding, first we must understand how html elements are viewed by the browser. Pretty much every html element has an invisible box around the content. Circular Image? It will have a square box with the circles diameter as the width and height.

Padding

Now, With the above in mind lets take a look at padding. Padding is the space between the content of the element, and the invisible box around the content.

To set padding in CSS it is very simple. Select the element you want to change using css, and add a padding attribute like this: p{padding-top: 5px;}. This will add a 5px space between the content and the invisible box the content sits in. We can add more padding by using other variations of the attribute, like padding-right or padding-bottom, but there is an easier way!
Enter, padding. You can use just "padding" to choose all 4 sides at the same time like this: p{padding:5px 10px 5px 10px;}. This adds 5px padding around the content on all sides.
If you are using equal padding for each side, you can just use p{padding: 5px;}, this will automatically apply the 5px to each side. In terms of knowing which number will impact which side of padding, its simple: Think clockwise starting at 12. Top, right, bottom, left.
So, in our first example the top and bottom padding is 5px and the left and right padding is 10px.
